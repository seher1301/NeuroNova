Week 2 11 June 2026

The Science of Dreams

Dreams are a series of images, thoughts, emotions, and sensations that occur during sleep. Although scientists still don't fully understand why we dream, research suggests that dreams may help with memory consolidation, emotional processing, and problem-solving.

Dreams are most vivid during REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep, when brain activity resembles that of a waking brain. During this stage, the eyes move rapidly, breathing becomes irregular, and most of the body's muscles are temporarily paralysed — preventing us from physically acting out our dreams.

Scientists have proposed several theories. One suggests dreams help strengthen and consolidate memories from the day. Another proposes that dreams allow the brain to process emotions in a safe environment. Some researchers believe dreams may help prepare us for future situations by simulating different scenarios.

Not all dreams are pleasant — nightmares are more common in children but can occur at any age, often triggered by stress, illness, or lack of sleep. Dreams remain one of the most fascinating mysteries of the human brain.

Week 1 4 June 2026

The Science of Memory

Memory is the process by which the brain encodes, stores, and retrieves information — happening through three main stages: Encoding, Storage, and Retrieval.

There are three types of memory: sensory memory (split-second data from your five senses), short-term memory (seconds to minutes, expandable through chunking and rehearsal), and long-term memory (vast capacity, potentially lasting a lifetime).

Long-term memory is further divided into declarative/explicit memory (knowing "what" — facts, events) and non-declarative/implicit memory (knowing "how" — skills, habits). When you ride a bike or tie your shoelaces, you're drawing on implicit memory without conscious thought.

Memory is not a perfect recording — each retrieval reconstructs the event from pieces, which means details can be altered, added, or lost over time. This is why eyewitness testimony can be unreliable, and why false memories are surprisingly common even in healthy individuals.

About NeuroNova

Hi! I'm Seher, a Grade 8 IGCSE student with a deep passion for neuroscience and psychology. NeuroNova started as a personal curiosity project — a way to explore the brain while helping others discover just how fascinating the mind can be.

Every fact, article, and resource on this site is researched and written by me. My goal is to make neuroscience feel exciting, relevant, and approachable — whether you're encountering it for the first time or already know the basics.

NeuroNova is growing into something bigger: a booklet, school workshops, and a long-term platform for young people interested in the brain sciences.
